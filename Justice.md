- Opportunities to function
- Enables basic capabilities achievement
- All individuals should achieve core capabilities
- Human development focus
- Quality of life emphasis

- **Rawls**: Patterned distribution favoring disadvantaged
- **Nozick**: Historical process, no pattern
- **Capabilities**: Functional freedoms matter
- Different philosophical foundations
- Varying implications for public policy
- State role conception differs
- Balance of liberty and equality
- Individual vs collective emphasis

**Conclusion:**
- Debate broad and diverse
- Range of philosophical foundations
- Implications for public policy
- Explores ethical dimensions
- How societies distribute resources
- Question central to political philosophy
- Design of equitable societies
- Justice remains contested concept
- Contemporary relevance continues

## Q4. Entitlement Theory of Justice (2022, 15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Expounded by Robert Nozick
- From "Anarchy, State, and Utopia" (1974)
- Unique libertarian perspective
- Constructed on three foundational principles
- **Nozick's axiom**: "Individuals have rights"
- "Things no person or group may do without violating rights"
- Individual rights paramount
- Anti-redistributive stance

**Body:**

*Principle 1 - Just Acquisition:*
- Individuals entitled to acquire property
- Through legitimate means
- **Methods**: Voluntary exchanges, own labor, gifts
- Must not infringe others' rights
- **Influenced by John Locke**: Natural right to property through labor
- Labor theory of value
- Original appropriation rules
- "Lockean proviso": Enough left for others
- Mixing labor with nature

*Principle 2 - Just Transfer:*
- Legitimacy of voluntary property transfers
- "Principle of justice in transfer"
- Rooted in individual autonomy
- **Nozick quote**: "Whatever arises from just situation by just steps is itself just"
- Voluntary exchange without coercion/fraud
- Resulting distribution considered just
- **Example**: Fair-market transaction
- Buying and selling car
- Recognizes just transfer of property rights
- Market outcomes legitimate if process fair
- No end-state pattern required

*Principle 3 - Rectification:*
- Addresses historical injustices
- Rights violations in past
- **Nozick on rectification**: "If past injustice rectified by transfer of holdings"
- "Holdings transferred must be of persons wronged"
- Not all acquisitions/transfers historically just
- Some resulted from force or fraud
- Rectification corrects specific past wrongs
- Does not endorse general redistribution
- Commitment to individual rights
- Property acquired justly is entitled
- Limited backward-looking correction

*Minimal State Concept:*
- Entails idea of minimal state
- "Night-watchman" state metaphor
- Limited role in society
- **Nozick's vision**: "Minimal state limited to narrow functions"
- "Protection against force, theft, fraud"
- "Enforcement of contracts, and so on"
- "Is inspiring as well as right"
- Primary duty: Safeguard rights
- Life, liberty, property protection
- No extensive regulatory activities
- No redistributive programs
- Avoid infringing on entitlements

*Anti-Redistributive Argument:*
- Taxation for redistribution is forced labor
- Takes fruits of one's labor
- Violates self-ownership
- Each person owns themselves
- State has no right to redistribute
- Even for worthy social goals
- Liberty trumps equality
- Process matters over outcomes

**Conclusion:**
- Provides clear and coherent framework
- Understanding justice through entitlement
- Subject of extensive debate
- Critics: May result in extreme inequalities
- Insufficiently addresses systemic issues
- Wealth distribution problems
- Social justice concerns
- Nonetheless continues to influence
- Discussions on political philosophy
- Justice theory debates
- Distinctive perspective on individual rights
- Property entitlements foundation
